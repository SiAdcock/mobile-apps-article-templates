Diss means war: Drake, Meek Mill and the grand tradition of hip-hop beefs

The two rappers spent the summer trading insults – the latest in the rich history of rap fallouts, from MC Busy Bee v Kool Moe Dee to Nas v Jay Z

Ben Westhoff

Published: 14:40 GMT Thursday, 29 October 2015

Follow Ben Westhoff Following Ben Westhoff

Sometimes, in rap, being successful in your own right is not enough. Others must also be seen to be in your shadow. In July, Meek Mill accused fellow rapper Drake of employing a ghostwriter, a serious charge in a genre that prides itself on creativity and self-expression. The criticism led to diss songs and insults levied across social media, not to mention mixed emotions from Mill’s girlfriend, Nicki Minaj, who shares a label with Drake.

In the end, Drake won the battle. His response tracks were more effective, and he has since moved on to hits such as Hotline Bling and its meme-worthy video. Meek Mill, however, won’t let things go, and is now embroiled in a spinoff beef with his own labelmate Wale.

The question is: Why did Meek Mill bother in the first place? After all, before taunting Drake, he had just released his chart-topping second album, Dreams Worth More Than Money, and his popularity was soaring. Now, however, there’s speculation that the Philadelphian’s rap career could be over.

Beef in hip-hop is nearly as old as the genre itself. Rap battles often play out like boxing matches, culminating in a devastating punchline akin to a knock-out blow. Yes, rap disputes sometimes escalate into real-life violence – as with Biggie and Tupac – but what’s most often on the line is the artists’ pride. Still, there is a lot of that in this bombastic genre; without pride, a rapper’s career is as good as over.

Rap beef has its roots in the traditional African-American insult game the dozens, in which participants try to get the better of each other through disrespectful, humorous barbs. That morphed into formalised rap battles, often with big money at stake, in which MCs traded off rhymes, showcasing their verbal dexterity and looking for creative ways to put down their opponent (or their opponent’s mother).

The best known early hip-hop battle happened during a contest at a New York club in 1981, when a party-starting MC named Busy Bee performed his light-hearted act. Back then, the competition was about who could best get the crowd hyped, and Busy pre-emptively declared himself the victor, even going so far as to pose with the trophy. But rapper Kool Moe Dee promptly became involved and changed the objective. Rather than trying to rock the crowd, he took the fight to his competition, challenging Busy Bee with a series of insults: his style was passe, his skills were inferior, he’d stolen his name from someone else. “If you was money, man, you’d be counterfeit,” Kool Moe Dee rapped.

This might not sound too harsh today, but it ushered in a new style. Now things were personal. When the Bronx act Boogie Down Productions took on Queens group Juice Crew in the mid-80s, in a lyrical dispute over hip-hop’s birthplace, the battle was considered to have ended the career of the Juice Crew’s MC Shan.

Things got darker following NWA’s splintering in the late 80s. Ice Cube threatened his old mates on the diss track No Vaseline, and when Dr Dre left the group a few years later, he partnered with Snoop Dogg and threatened to murder his former colleague Eazy-E on Dre Day. Eazy-E rebounded even more aggressively on the sinister Real Compton City G’s.

These records conveyed serious tension and raw emotion. It all felt real, which helped drive the artists’ sales to great heights. But, at a time when Crips and Bloods ruled many Los Angeles neighborhoods, the gang mentality was permeating hip-hop. This was particularly true at the infamous label Death Row Records, co-founded by Suge Knight.

The imprint signed Tupac Shakur on his 1995 release from prison, and he and Knight instigated the most frightening rap beef in the genre’s history, taking on Biggie Smalls and his New York-based label, Bad Boy. This so-called east coast-west coast beef – which was mostly just between the two labels – culminated in the deaths of Tupac and Biggie in 1996 and 1997, respectively. The murders remain unsolved.

Thankfully, a relative state of calm set in afterwards. Since then, with scattered exceptions, deadly hostility has been mostly absent from the genre in the nearly two decades since. But that doesn’t mean rap beef has been taken off the boil. Following Biggie’s death, two other New York City MCs, Jay Z and Nas, began battling over who was the true heir to his throne. The battle produced a pair of classic, caustic tracks – Jay Z’s The Takeover and Nas’s Ether – which took aim at each other’s styles, crews and sexuality, among other subjects. “You ain’t live it / You witnessed it from your folks’ pad,” Jay alleged, questioning Nas’s street credentials. They brought out the best in each other lyrically, and no one died.

It’s great to have dynamic wordplay, a fluid delivery and motivational messages, but personal attacks are often an effective shortcut to hip-hop glory. 50 Cent broke out on the strength of his 1999 track How to Rob, which laid out in shockingly specific detail exactly what he would do to whom in the rap and R&B communities. “I’ll snatch Kim and tell Puff: ‘You want to see her again / Get your ass down to the nearest ATM,’” he raps, referencing Lil Kim and the producer now known as Diddy. The track reinforced 50 Cent’s fearless image, and he would go on to become embroiled in numerous battles with other MCs, usually at his own instigation. His track record is mixed, however; he’s credited with KO-ing Ja Rule and diminishing Fat Joe, but couldn’t best Kanye West or Rick Ross. Nowadays, 50 is all but commercially irrelevant as a rapper.

It demonstrates just how high-stakes these battles can be – whether they take place on wax or, increasingly, on Twitter. Beef can also be a driving force pushing MCs to be more creative. To extend the boxing metaphor, it offers upstarts the opportunity to take on champions. That’s probably what Meek Mill had in mind when he came at Drake with the ghostwriting allegations. (He also may have been a bit jealous that Drake and Minaj have long been flirtatious.) Sure, Meek Mill was already successful, but he wasn’t a household name like Drake, and he was clearly bothered by his suspicion that the latter wasn’t playing by hip-hop’s rules.

Unfortunately for him, no one seemed to care much. After all, plenty of revered rappers have had help writing their songs over the years. In Drake’s case, he even tended to give credit to his co-writers, and pay them royalties. And so, in the meantime, like a skilled politician, Drake turned around and made the dispute a referendum on Mill’s abilities. “All y’all stare in my face in hopes you could be the replacement,” Drake rapped on his track Charged Up. Mill’s response, when it finally came, was too little, too late.

But that’s not to say Meek Mill shouldn’t have picked the fight. Many more people are now familiar with him than before the feud started, and he may be able to leverage the increased attention to his advantage. At the very least, the back and forth was entertaining for fans, an ideological battle that raised important questions about hip-hop as an artform. And, of course, everyone kept their guns in their holsters.
