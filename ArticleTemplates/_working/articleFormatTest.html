The US president was also asked why France did not share in the same intelligence co-operation with Washington that the Brits do if it is now such a close and trusted ally. But once again Obama was rescued by his charming guest, who glossed over European anger toward US surveillance practices and implied all was forgiven – in the Élysée Palace at least. “We wanted to fight against terrorism, but we also wanted to meet a number of principles. And we are making headway in this cooperation. Mutual trust has been restored,” Hollande said.

In the end, only language could come between the two men otherwise bound together by domestic travails. “Good morning, everybody. Bonjour. That’s the extent of my French,” Obama announced as he welcomed Hollande to the chilly White House portico. “Few places warm the heart like Paris in the spring,” he added. “Today we’re going to do our best with Washington in the winter.”

It had not gone unnoticed by the French contingent. “It is cold in Washington,” replied Hollande in his few words of English. Even the New York Times reporter apologised for his lack of French – in French – while the large pack of French reporters had to be told off for breaking decorum and taking selfies in the Oval Office.

But the first state visit by a French president in nearly 20 years clearly proved a useful enough distraction for both men to demand a rematch. Obama announced he will be going to France in June for the 70th anniversary of D-Day.
