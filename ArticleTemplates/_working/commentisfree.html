Prime minister's questions is a great British export we need more of, not less Anne Perkins

 Follow Contributor  Unfollow Contributor
Calls for reform of PMQs miss the point. The weekly clash reminds us of the essential nature of politics, and that opposing views are worth fighting for

10:49 GMT Wednesday, 26 February 2014

 24 Comments

Anne Perkins

It was half-term for MPs last week, so instead of prime minister's questions, there was a flurry of proposals for how it might be reformed. There are people, many people, who believe political discourse can be conducted in an atmosphere of enlightened calm. At noon today, we will see whether anything at all has changed. I think we know the answer. Phew.

The this-is-bad-for-parliament trope has been a regular since PMQs was introduced more than 50 years ago. The latest cry for change was triggered by a report from the Hansard Society, which as well as publishing the Commons debates is a thinktank dedicated to promoting parliament and democracy. It conducted a survey that found that most viewers thought the weekly half-hour of questions was completely pointless. They thought it was worse than a playground riot and about as much use in holding David Cameron to account. It was an exercise variously described as "pathetic", "point scoring" and "pantomime". What they wanted was calm, rational debate. They miss the point of PMQs, but maybe that's because they miss the point of politics.

Prime minister's questions is a great British export. It holds viewers enthralled across the world, and also here, though possibly because many are in some kind of rigor provoked by rage. More than half of the Hansard survey respondents who were so critical of the event had actually watched some of PMQs in the past year, which must be more than most TV viewers can claim. What they mostly watch is the exchanges between the leaders, probably on the news. Apart from that, though – as the few who do stick with it for the whole 30 minutes would accept – PMQs can be calm to the point of torpor, interesting only to students of the granularity of politics. This is, however, when prime ministers can learn quite useful stuff, like how long it takes to get to see a GP, or exactly how angry voters are about flooding or bank bonuses or the government's reluctance to reveal which employers don't pay the minimum wage. What do you mean, you missed that?

So when it comes down to it, what people really dislike, including the players themselves, is that seven or eight minutes when Cameron and Ed Miliband go head to head. That's because it's not understood for what it is. Consider it like this. Each player's objective is to manage the exchanges so that they can use them as a platform for a distillation of their point of view. At the same time, they have to find a way to put the other under the kind of pressure that will lure them into behaviour that leaves them looking foolish/ignorant/bullying/weak or in any other way inferior. This requires an intelligent understanding of the other's position, some nimbleness of expression and the ability to look unruffled, however panicked you feel.

This clash is conducted either with or across the rowdy support of a backbench chorus whose role is generally limited to degrees of volume with the odd Frankie Howerd-like expression of wonderment. What TV viewers don't know, because microphones lift the voices above the general noise, is how incredibly loud it is in the chamber. Politicians recall this row as a kind of animal, capable of being terrifying in its hostility. But this is part of the ordeal. There is no hiding place here, no opportunity to disguise a loss of nerve, a place that has to be dominated in order to find space to think.

The result is not always edifying. On bad days, Cameron and Miliband just shout at each other. But it's not the system that is wrong, it's them. They haven't thought hard enough about what they want to achieve, or about how to do it. Yelling is the easy option in a fight, like flouncing out and slamming the door. The smart thing to do is to develop a strategy and stay and fight for it. Politics is not a process by which every difference can be negotiated away. Sometimes there are irreconcilable differences.

There are a lot of things wrong with parliament. But the only thing that needs changing about prime minister's questions is that there isn't enough of it. Return it to the old twice-a-week format. Less time, more often. Make the party leaders keep up with what's happening around them. Remind the rest of us that our leaders might look like peas in a pod, but there are differences between them that matter.
