Preamble

Team USA’s upcoming encounter against Canada is not only the marquee matchup of a day of men’s Olympic ice hockey semifinals, but arguably, it’s the game of the tournament - there’s no need for any of us to pretend that it’s not. However, we’d all be making a big mistake by minimizing Sweden and Finland’s hockey credentials, and their very own Scandinavian rivalry, which is one of the strongest in the game. Fact is, on Sunday, either one of these teams are capable of walking out of Sochi’s Bolshoy Ice Dome wearing gold around their necks - Sweden have won it the tournament twice in their previous six Olympic tournaments, while Finland have medaled in four of the last five Games, with three of those medals coming since NHLers joined the fray, the most of any nation. So yes, there is some serious hockey about to come our way, but since we can’t resist putting every little thing in life into a nice tidy little box, let’s just agree just call this game a 1b on today’s doubleheader and move on.

Deal?

OK.

When you consider that Finland was once actually part of Sweden, for a very, very, very long time, you begin to understand just why there’s more to this rivalry other than just two countries that happen to share a border and play the same game. Still, the steady Scandinavian mind set prevents matters from getting completely out of control, with players such as Sweden’s Daniel Alfredsson keeping it cool.

I think it was a more intense rivalry in the past than it is now. I think now it’s a healthy, friendly rivalry, whereas they used to look up to us as big brother, but they’ve had some great success...they’ve had tremendous success here in the Olympics, we’ve met them pretty much every time, and I don’t think it’s so much big brother/little brother any more.

Maybe yes, maybe no. When Sweden’s head coach Par Marts was asked who would win the quarterfinal game between Finland and Russia, Marts did something we’re not used to seeing. He answered the question, honestly. “Russia” he replied, providing a little pre-game lockerroom fodder for the Finns.

That’s a snapshot of some of the mental aspects heading into this particular game, but what about on the ice? Well, the rumor around Sochi is that Sweden have yet to play their best game of the tournament, which means if they did happen to put it all together, Finland would quickly find themselves on their back skate. Captain Niklas Kronwall, goalie Henrik Lundqvist, who anchored the 2006 gold medal team that defeated Finland in the final, and Erik Karlsson are part of a talented group generally seen to be more gifted than the Finns. Then again, almost everybody is seen to be more skillful that the Finns, and yet, Suomi are always in the mix. This time around, they’ve done it despite major injuries that have put players such as 43-year-old Teemu Selanne back into the forefront - the future Hall-of-Famer has responded by putting up four points for his club. Tuukka Rask has been phenomenal in net for his country and he and Lundqvist have probably been the best two goalies in the tournament not named Florence Schelling.

That means this game could very well be a tight, low scoring affair, but we’ll find out if that theory holds any frozen water soon enough - the puck will drop at 700AM EST and noon in the UK.
